# Exercise Coaching Notes — Common Form Mistakes & Cues

These notes give the AI coach additional context when diagnosing technique issues from
joint-angle data. Each section covers the most common errors, what the angle data will
show, and the verbal cue to use.

---

## Squat

### Common Mistakes

**1. Knee cave (valgus collapse)**
- What it looks like: Knees track inward on descent or ascent.
- What the angle data shows: Knee angle may look fine, but hip adduction is happening
  outside the tracker's view. If the athlete mentions knees caving, angle data alone
  won't catch it — flag it verbally.
- Cue: "Push your knees out over your pinky toes." / "Screw your feet into the ground."

**2. Not reaching depth**
- What it looks like: Knee angle stays above 100° (short of target 90°).
- What the angle data shows: Min angle is 105-120° when target is 80-100°.
- Cue: "Sit into the squat — drive hips between your heels." / "Chase the floor."
- Fix: Elevate heels slightly to reduce ankle mobility demand while building range.

**3. Forward torso lean (excessive)**
- What it looks like: Upper body pitches too far forward.
- What the angle data shows: Knee angle may reach target but the movement feels unbalanced.
- Cue: "Chest up. Pull your elbows down and back."
- Note: Some forward lean is normal. Excessive lean (torso below 45°) shifts load to back.

**4. Butt wink (posterior pelvic tilt at depth)**
- What it looks like: Pelvis tucks under at the bottom of the squat.
- What the angle data shows: Often appears when athlete chases depth below their current
  mobility threshold.
- Cue: "Stop 5° above where your butt winks. Build depth gradually."

## Bicep Curl

### Common Mistakes

**1. Elbow drifting forward (momentum cheat)**
- What it looks like: Elbow moves forward at the top of the curl.
- What the angle data shows: Max angle may exceed 160° (past full contraction) because
  the shoulder is flexing to compensate.
- Cue: "Keep your elbow pinned at your side. Only the forearm moves."

**2. Incomplete range of motion**
- What it looks like: Not fully extending at the bottom (min angle stays above 50°).
- What the angle data shows: Min angle 50-80° instead of target 30-40°.
- Cue: "Fully straighten at the bottom — feel the stretch in your bicep."

**3. Swinging / using momentum**
- What it looks like: Torso rocks back to help lift the weight.
- What the angle data shows: Very fast angle changes, inconsistent data, wide variance
  between min and max across samples.
- Cue: "Slow the negative down to 3 seconds. If you can't, the weight is too heavy."

## Shoulder Press

### Common Mistakes

**1. Elbow flare at the bottom**
- What it looks like: Elbows wider than wrists at the starting position.
- What the angle data shows: Starting angle may read higher than expected 90° target.
- Cue: "Tuck your elbows slightly forward — think 'elbows in front of the bar.'"

**2. Lumbar hyperextension (arching back)**
- What it looks like: Lower back arches excessively to compensate for shoulder mobility.
- What the angle data shows: Athlete may reach the 170° target but through spinal extension,
  not shoulder range. Ask if they feel it in their lower back.
- Cue: "Brace your core hard. Glutes squeezed. Press straight up."

**3. Not reaching full lockout**
- What it looks like: Elbow angle stays below 165° at the top.
- What the angle data shows: Max angle 140-160° when target is 170°.
- Cue: "Shrug your traps at the top and press to full lockout."
- Fix: Reduce load. Incomplete lockout at overhead press is a load-management issue.

## Push-Up

### Common Mistakes

**1. Sagging hips**
- What it looks like: Hips drop below the plane of the body during descent.
- What the angle data shows: Arm angle may be fine, but form is compromised above the waist.
- Cue: "Squeeze your glutes and brace your abs. Body = one long board."

**2. Flared elbows**
- What it looks like: Elbows point outward (90° from torso) rather than angled back (~45°).
- What the angle data shows: Elbow angle at bottom may appear shallow even if form is poor.
- Cue: "Point your elbows back toward your hips — not out to the sides."

**3. Not reaching bottom**
- What it looks like: Chest doesn't reach near the floor.
- What the angle data shows: Min elbow angle stays above 90° when target is 75-80°.
- Cue: "Lower until your chest grazes the floor. Slow and controlled."
- Fix: Try incline push-ups to build strength through full range.

## Deadlift

### Common Mistakes

**1. Rounding the lower back**
- What it looks like: Lumbar flexion during the pull.
- What the angle data shows: Hip angle at setup may appear correct but the spine is
  loaded in flexion — a serious injury risk. If athlete mentions back rounding, flag urgently.
- Cue: "Chest proud. Lats tight — think 'protect your armpits.'"
- Note: This is the most important cue in deadlifting. Never proceed with back rounding.

**2. Bar drifting away from body**
- What it looks like: Bar swings outward during the pull.
- What the angle data shows: May not appear in angle data — cue verbally if athlete mentions it.
- Cue: "Drag the bar up your shins. Scrape your legs on the way up."

**3. Hip shooting up (stiff-leg deadlift pattern)**
- What it looks like: Hips rise before the bar breaks the floor.
- What the angle data shows: Hip angle increases very rapidly from starting position.
- Cue: "Push the floor away — think leg press, not back pull."

**4. No lockout**
- What it looks like: Hip angle doesn't reach full extension at the top.
- What the angle data shows: Max hip angle below 165° when target is 175°+.
- Cue: "Drive hips through at the top. Stand tall — don't hyperextend back."

## General Coaching Philosophy

- Always give one positive observation alongside any correction.
- Reference the specific week's angle targets when giving numeric feedback.
- One main cue per set — don't overwhelm the athlete with multiple corrections simultaneously.
- If data shows wide variance (inconsistent samples), suspect fatigue or load too heavy.
- A single rep outside the range is noise. A pattern across many samples is signal.
